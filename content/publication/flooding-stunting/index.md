abstract: 'Nigeria''s 774 Local Government Areas (LGAs) are the administrative units most directly responsible for delivering nutrition, health, and social protection programmes, yet standard household surveys cannot produce reliable estimates of child stunting at this level of granularity. This paper proceeds in two steps. First, I produce LGA-level estimates of child stunting prevalence by fitting Bayesian spatial small-area estimation models (BYM2 area-level models with covariates, estimated with R-INLA) to anthropometric data from multiple rounds of the Demographic and Health Survey (2003–2018), complemented by LSMS and MICS microdata. Second, I exploit the catastrophic 2012 Nigeria floods as a natural experiment: combining satellite-derived flood exposure measures with the SAE estimates, I estimate the impact of flood exposure on child stunting in a matched difference-in-differences design, with propensity-score matching on pre-flood LGA characteristics and controls for time-varying climatic and socioeconomic conditions. The results document how large-scale flooding deteriorates children''s nutritional status and highlight the value of granular, survey-plus-satellite data for targeting climate-resilient nutrition and social protection policies.'
